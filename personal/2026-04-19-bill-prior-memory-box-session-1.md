# Bill Prior — Memory Box Session 1

## TL;DR

Ford's father Bill Prior was born in 1957 in Augusta, GA (his parents chose Georgia over South Carolina for the hospital delivery as a matter of state pride) and grew up in Aiken, SC. This recording captures a wide-ranging conversation about his grandfather Dr. Baker, childhood and prep school experiences, the racial environment of the Jim Crow-era South, his family's philosophy of generosity and community care, advice on life partners and mentors, and the spiritual experience of losing his friend Betty to ALS. Recorded August 2, 2022 at Common House Richmond.

## Context

This is the first Memory Box recording, a project conceived by Ford and Bridget Prior to capture family stories on audio for posterity. The questions were written on small cards and drawn from a box. Participants: Bill Prior (Ford's father) and Ford Prior. Setting: Common House Richmond recording studio, ~8:57pm on August 2, 2022. Both had been working all day — Bill at Fidelity National (IT), Ford at Amazon (working from Common House's co-working space).

The "Memory Box" itself was a Christmas morning gift containing question cards from Ford and Bridget, originally meant to be recorded in January/February 2022. Scheduled dates were missed, and the recording happened eight months later.

## Key Topics Covered

### Origins & Birth

- **Born**: 1957, **Augusta, Georgia** — though the family lived in **Aiken, South Carolina**
- Augusta was the closest real hospital (~15 minutes away)
- **Why Georgia**: Bill's parents were from Georgia (his mother from Atlanta). They didn't want a South Carolina birth certificate. They also did most of their shopping in Augusta — a quiet state-pride preference. "They thought everything was a little better in Georgia."
- This extended to college: Bill's parents were amazed/amused when he chose **College of Charleston** (South Carolina) over Georgia schools like Suwannee, Emory, or Georgia Tech

### College of Charleston

- Bill attended despite parents' mild Georgia loyalties
- The school was "so distracting with sailing, water, beaches" — and yet he graduated
- Bill's father (Dr. Baker) loved visiting: would drive the ~3-hour round trip, often just for the day, driving back that evening without staying overnight
- Dr. Baker reportedly wanted to stay — "like, next week" — and would have moved there if he could

### The Prior/Baker Family Dynamic

**Dr. Baker (Bill's father, Ford's grandfather):**
- A **physician** in Aiken, SC
- **Stoic**: not a lot of smiling, minimal verbal warmth; but expressed love through action and showing up
- Brilliant communicator with a very keen mind; loved talking about stars and science
- Would get "in close" and walk family members through things patiently; got more patient as grandchildren arrived
- Was the company doctor for the **Granville Textile Mill** — on staff as a mill employee doctor; had a small strip-mall office
- Did **house calls**, delivered babies in people's homes, drove around serving mill workers
- People in Aiken and Augusta treated him "like a celebrity" — Bill would encounter former patients who revered his father
- Believed in the **community doctor model** (socialized medicine at the community level: one doctor, one barber, a few beauty salons, everyone cares for everyone)
- Had a **generous heart for the downtrodden** despite a somewhat formal exterior
- As a heart surgeon, he carried the weight of life-and-death decisions; his family was sometimes hard on his stoicism without crediting what he'd given
- **Example**: Dr. Baker was the working doctor for the textile mill. A mill worker named **Mose** (husband of Sarah, the family's housekeeper) lost his arm in a mill machine. Dr. Baker amputated it, saved Mose's life, and then **employed Mose** doing yard work and general tasks for the family for years afterward. He also continued making house calls to care for Mose and Sarah's **12 children**.

**Bill's parents — driving habits:**
- Both parents loved driving and were not bothered by long drives
- Bill identified this as possibly a generational trait — their generation just liked moving around
- Bill thinks he picked up a mild version of this ("wild hair up my ass — gotta go, time to move")
- Ford notes he has it too, which Bill found interesting
- Bill's father liked his own space — probably drove the pattern of short visits

**Family summers:**
- Bill's father would put Bill and his brother **Bruce** on a bus to **Dublin, Georgia** for weeks in the summer to stay with their grandfather, while the sisters went to Atlanta to stay with their mom's parents and shop
- At their grandfather's farm in Dublin: would ride in the back of a **blue Ford pickup**, swim in cold-water springs while grandfather smoked a cigar and watched stoically, drive a tractor — "cur dogs in the back of a pickup truck"
- Grandfather wasn't overtly affectionate but showed love by taking them in and giving them freedom

**Generational masculinity:**
- Bill reflects that men of his grandfather's generation didn't raise children the way modern fathers do — less nurturing, more stoic
- "There was a lot of just put on your pants every day and go do what you needed to do"
- Moments of joy were real — a good meal, holding a baby — but not emotionally expansive
- His own father (Dr. Baker, born 1929) lived through the Depression and grew up in the WWII era; learned from somewhere (possibly "Dr. Baker" — the elder) to be a **better communicator** than his own father

**Ford's grandmother's Berlin story:**
- Ford's maternal grandmother saw Hitler's pamphlets/leaflets as a child when her family was in **Berlin** (father was teaching at a German university as an adjunct)
- Left before things got too dangerous; Kristallnacht happened about six months after they left
- She was ~5 years old; one of her core memories was **almost being abducted** by a man on the street — a neighbor stopped him
- She later had a book with Hitler propaganda leaflets from that trip
- Bill reflects this story was probably his grandmother recounting her own mother's memory, since she was so young

### Aiken Prep School & Race

**Context — Aiken in the Civil Rights era:**
- Colored water fountains, second-class treatment of Black residents; no overt rage but a pervasive "they don't need to be talked about, they're just second class" attitude
- After MLK's assassination, race riots broke out in Augusta
- Dr. Baker imagined retribution — understood it was deserved — and was genuinely frightened
- He sent **Bill and Bruce to Aiken Prep** specifically to keep them out of the newly integrated public schools, where he expected violence between white and Black students (and he was right — there were frequent fights)
- Sisters were not sent to Aiken Prep

**Aiken Prep:**
- An unusual old **English-style classical boarding/day school** next door to the hospital where Dr. Baker worked
- Dr. Baker could look out of the hospital's top floor and watch Bill playing soccer — that was how he decided it was a good school
- Classical curriculum: Latin, European history, Greek mythology ("Veritas"-style classical education)
- Day students (including Bill) attended, with wealthy boarders from families like the Acres (trucking) and Clawsons (bread)
- At 11am: a student would retrieve a **tray of milk and cookies** for the room; at 11:30am, all students changed into pajamas and took a **mandatory rest**; then got up into gym clothes and did afternoon activities
- Activities: soccer, softball, **bicycle polo** (very hard on bikes; they had a communal pile of bike parts and would rebuild bikes), Riflery in a shooting range under the gym (old mattresses, .22 rifles, targets down a tunnel), swimming, **tunnel club** (digging tunnels — ended when friend Birdie Phipps got buried alive in a cave-in at ~age 13 and had to be dug out), horse riding through Hitchcock Woods, golf, train clubs, rocket clubs
- "Nobody else can understand Aiken Prep. It was like muggle Hogwarts."
- The dorm mother: sat inside in sunglasses watching boys walk by, described as "creepy" — likely a factor in why the school eventually ended the dormitory system
- Nine students per class; Bill was one of the "poor" day students relative to the ultra-wealthy boarders
- Bill's best friends there: Niles Borup, John O'Neill, David McEwan, Bruce (his brother)

**Community care of the mentally ill:**
- Bill recalls a man in Aiken who was "clearly mentally ill" — someone in the community gave him the job of running people through the car wash
- This model — community quietly absorbing and employing vulnerable people — stuck with Bill as an ideal
- He and Ford discuss the limits of this model: works when everyone knows each other and can relate; breaks down at scale or the moment someone crosses a line; inherently unreliable; but very beautiful when it works
- "It was an unseen system — a moral framework that was being thrived upon"

### Advice & Wisdom

**On choosing a life partner:**
> "Take the selection of your life partner very seriously. I feel so blessed that I found Claire. When I go back to Aiken, I can very well imagine who I would have ended up with instead — and that's nightmarish."

**On finding a mentor:**
- Bill didn't find a mentor the way he wished he had
- **Jack Mushaw**: encouraged Bill to get into tech, made it sound exciting — this was before the internet, when early computer technology was "clunky and large, like the metaverse is now"
- Recommendation: find a mentor both for your career and for personal growth; find accountability partners you trust

**On moving to Richmond, Virginia:**
- Bill and Claire moved from South Carolina to Richmond when they didn't have to; nothing was pushing them
- He calls it one of the best decisions of his life — "one of the star features of being in a tech kind of career: it kept me mobile"
- Moving expanded their world in ways staying in Aiken never could have

**On health regret:**
- "I kind of blame myself for my poor heart health. I had all the information I needed. I knew my mom had all those heart conditions. I could have avoided that path — it was totally on me."
- Regrets not taking better care: diet, exercise, mental health
- Used to run, play soccer, use the YMCA
- "God gave me a nice, beautiful spiral of exactly what would happen if I did that — and I just ran right past it."

### Spiritual Life — Betty's Death

**The most profound spiritual experience of his life:**
- When his friend **Betty** died from **ALS** in ~2015 (one year after his father died in 2014)
- When Bill heard the news, he looked up at the sky on a bright September day and saw a **cloud formation**
- "I swear it was Betty. I could see her."
- Gives him chills to recall where he was and what he felt at that exact moment
- He describes it as one of the few moments he genuinely felt the divine: "I felt like I could see her"
- He hadn't been able to visit Betty as she got sicker; seeing her "in the cloud" felt like a goodbye he hadn't been able to say

**Bill's spirituality:**
- Has always struggled with the concept of predestination and a fixed plan
- Believes souls have weight and that something happens to that energy after death — science can't explain it but "something is working"
- Drawn to the idea that meaning is being settled in real time: "the universe is constantly expanding — meaning is constantly being written"
- All faiths seem to agree on a proper vs. improper way to live — that common thread feels meaningful to him
- When losing people he loves, he chooses to believe they are at peace: "That's where I want them to be, and that's where I'm going to choose to think."
- Ford's framing: "We're in an escape room together. Betty got out through the door. Everyone leaves through the door. Maybe it's better over there. Maybe it sucks. But we're all going to be there together — she just beat us to it."

### What He Wants to Be Remembered For

- Unconditional love for family — "no matter what they do"
- Fun, spontaneous, unpredictable
- "A sailor, a golfer, a madman — always down, always able to fix something or at least try"
- The person who would take you somewhere random: walk down a beach in a storm, find a swing in the woods, get coal off the railroad tracks, meet a random dog
- The person who, at Advanced Auto, will belly-laugh with you and the counter guy about windshield wipers
- **Regret**: "It wasn't weird enough." Looking back, he wishes he'd been weirder and more spontaneous, especially while raising kids — he was trying very hard to be "down the middle of the fairway"
- The dream for now: grab whoever will go, board **the Porvita** (their sailboat), and sail somewhere for a long time
- Grandparent role: deeply loves his grandkids (Thaddeus, Rio, Amelia, Jack, Beatrix); grandchildren "change you" — he became softer, more patient, more deliberate in explaining things to them (just like his own father did)

## Analysis

Bill Prior's personal narrative reveals a man shaped by three competing forces: a stoic, high-achieving physician father who modeled community duty without emotional expressiveness; a racially charged Southern landscape that forced him to reckon with injustice early; and a tech career that gave him mobility, identity, and economic freedom.

The most recurring themes in this session:
1. **Mentorship as a chain**: Dr. Baker → Bill → Ford. Each generation learns something the previous couldn't fully model and attempts to improve on it.
2. **Community care vs. systemic solutions**: The textile mill doctor, the car wash worker, Mose's continued employment — Bill grew up in a world where communities quietly absorbed vulnerable people. He's conflicted about whether this scales, but emotionally drawn to it.
3. **Spontaneity as identity**: His deepest regret isn't career or health — it's that he wasn't weird enough. He was conservative during child-rearing and now wishes he'd leaned harder into his chaotic, joyful instincts.
4. **Faith as a choice, not a certainty**: He doesn't have traditional doctrinal faith; he has chosen to believe in peace after death because the alternative is unbearable. This is a pragmatic, emotionally honest spirituality.

### Notable Direct Quotes

On his grandfather's stoicism at the swimming hole:
> "He would just pull over and be like, 'All right, jump in, go for a swim.' And he'd smoke his cigar. And we would go in these freezing springs, these cold water springs that would come bubbling up around Dublin... I like to imagine he would smoke his cigar and just watch you guys and think, 'I was young once.' And then just — that's it, no drama. Just — that's a beautiful thing."

On the word "leader":
> "I think of my dad. He had such a big, diverse group in my little family of five kids, and he led it and did it the way he thought was best, and he had a vision for it... He didn't have a ton of empathy — until later in life. Maybe [needed] a little more self-awareness. But he was a good leader in spite of those shortcomings. He overcame."

On regrets for future generations:
> "As a generation, I think we've done a disservice in many, many things that were selfish. We gave you the Trump family, the Bidens, all of the leaders you have right now. We also screwed up the planet where we should have done better. So — other people kind of screwed it up — but what could I have done better? I know you don't like to spend time regretting..."
