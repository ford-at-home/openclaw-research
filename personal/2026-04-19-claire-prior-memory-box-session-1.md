# Claire Prior — Memory Box Session 1

## TL;DR

Ford's mother Claire Prior was born March 7, 1959 in Aiken, SC — the youngest of four girls, daughter of a DuPont mechanical engineer who was part of the Cold War "bomb plant" influx that transformed Aiken in the 1950s. Her childhood was shaped by her parents' divorce at ~age 5-6, a difficult relationship with an unstable mother, a series of grounding women figures (Eva, Mrs. Brown), a deep faith, and her father's peaceful death from leukemia that became the defining spiritual moment of her life. She married Bill Prior in her mid-20s, moved to Richmond, raised three kids, worked at St. Christopher's School, and describes herself as a woman of faith who loves reading, biking, swimming, tennis, and quiet.

## Context

This is the first Memory Box recording with Claire Prior (Ford's mother). Format mirrors the session with Bill Prior — questions drawn from a box, mostly recorded as a flowing conversation. Ford facilitates. The recording appears to be from approximately 2022-2023, recorded at Common House Richmond or a similar setting.

## Key Topics Covered

### Origins & Birth

- **Born**: March 7, 1959, **Aiken, South Carolina**
- Youngest of four girls; her sisters were significantly older:
  - Susan (oldest, 13 years older than Claire)
  - One more sister (~10 years older)
  - Deb (~9 years older)
  - Laurie (~9 years older)
- "I had three additional mothers in the house when I was born"
- Father: **mechanical engineer at DuPont** — moved to Aiken when the "bomb plant" was built in 1953
- Mother: background in Philadelphia and Woodstown, New Jersey (where DuPont had previously employed her father)

### The DuPont Era in Aiken

- DuPont's **Savannah River Site** ("the bomb plant") was built in 1953 to produce plutonium and other nuclear materials for the Cold War
- DuPont recruited large numbers of engineers and employees from the Northeast, particularly Philadelphia/New Jersey
- **Entire new neighborhoods were built** around Aiken to house the influx — Claire's family lived on **Oleander Drive** (one of these new neighborhoods)
- Claire's aunt and uncle also came down with DuPont but left after a year — found it too hot with too many mosquitoes, too different from the North
- Claire's father stayed at DuPont for his **entire career** — the only place he ever worked
- He likely retired around age 60-63, meaning ~35-40 years at DuPont
- Work schedule: carpooled with other engineers, arrived at 7:30am, home by 4-4:30pm; summers meant he was often at the pool or playing tennis after work
- He applied his mechanical engineering skills at home too — loved keeping up a house and pool
- **The "northerners" vs. "Aikenites":** A cultural divide existed, though not overtly hostile. The DuPont crowd brought in skilled workers, better teachers, and civic investment. "The Aikenites were a little in awe." It bettered the community.

### The Fermata Club

- Claire's father was one of the **founding members** of the Fermata Club
- The Fermata Club was an old, run-down **music conservatory building** on Whiskey Road in Aiken
- He and other DuPont dads converted it into a **swimming pool and tennis club**
- Claire did not know this until adulthood
- He was there every Saturday — his relaxation spot; he oversaw it with care (he eventually kept up two pools — this one, and his own pool after the divorce on Whitney Drive)
- The club had a ballroom (from its conservatory days); the DuPont crowd held **Saturday afternoon tea dances**
- Claire's father met **Amy** (his later partner/wife) at one of those tea dances
- The club became a place where Claire could observe healthy family life — "what it looks like to be in a family" — and instilled her deep love of the pool as a site of community

### Parents' Divorce

- Claire's parents divorced when she was approximately **5-6 years old**, while she was still in kindergarten on Oleander Drive
- She has clear memories of life on Oleander Drive before the divorce (neighbor families, kindergarten teacher Mrs. Hunter living next door)
- After the divorce:
  - Claire and her mother moved to **Kershaw Street** (a more "Aikenite" area)
  - Sister Deb went to live with their father
  - Sister Laurie lived with Claire and their mother through a turbulent period; got pregnant at 15 and was sent to a home in Charleston (which Laurie later described as a good experience — nice food, peaceful environment — given the turmoil at home)
  - Susan was already in or near college age
- Claire did not like Kershaw Street: no kids her age nearby, had to go through woods and hop a fence to find children to play with
- Her mother was emotionally unpredictable: "I never knew what I was going to get each day."
- "She made my life pretty miserable, day in and day out. There were times when she was okay."

### Claire's Mother — Background and Origins

Claire pieced together the story of her mother's difficult history:
- Her mother's father **left the family** when Claire's mother was approximately 9-10 years old — "disappeared with his secretary," eventually ending up in California starting a peanut business
- Her mother's mother was an **alcoholic** who "threw chairs"
- After her father left, Claire's mother, her sisters, and younger brother eventually got **kicked out by their grandmother** and lived in an apartment together in their teens — the older sisters working to support the family, looking after the younger brother (~age 12)
- **Possible sexual abuse**: Claire believes there may have been an uncle who came to visit and went into the girls' room; she doesn't know details but the shape of her mother's behavior suggested something traumatic beyond the father leaving
- Bill Prior (Claire's future husband) dated Claire's mother throughout this period — she thinks he may have felt like he was "saving" her
- Her parents married after Bill got out of the Navy
- Claire's mother asked for the divorce; she was not abandoned
- After the divorce, her mother tried to interfere with Claire's relationship with her father:
  - Would find reasons at the last minute why Claire couldn't see him
  - Would leave notes on the door for her father when he pulled up
  - Claire could see him from her window, stuck in her room, unable to go out
  - Would interrogate Claire when she returned from seeing her father, then get moody
  - "I felt like I had to sneak over to see my dad"
- Once Claire moved to Richmond after marriage, she was able to set firm boundaries — her mother "kind of cut her off" in response, but Claire held firm, as Bridget (Ford's wife) has done as an adult

### Grounding Figures in a Difficult Childhood

**Eva:**
- The family's housekeeper (referred to at the time as "the maid")
- Claire describes her as "gentle, kind" — a consistent, safe presence
- Claire would sit at Eva's feet while Eva ironed, finding comfort in her presence
- "I found real comfort just being in her presence"
- She rarely remembers her mother being in the picture during these moments — mother was possibly in her room, isolating, smoking, or crying

**Mrs. Brown:**
- An elderly English woman (in her 80s) living "through a little patch of woods" from Kershaw Street
- Spent winters in Aiken, summers in Maine (possibly)
- Every weekday at **4:00pm sharp** (not before 4), children could come to her yard — it was a structured, open, loving invitation
- Activities: putt-putt on one side of the yard, croquet on the other, ping pong in the garage, basketball hoop in the field — all free
- Mrs. Brown would be out there the whole time, present with the kids
- At **5:30pm** ("half past five"), everyone went home — no wiggle room, firm boundary
- On rainy days only: children could come inside; two rooms only — a room with a magnificent multi-story **dollhouse** with furniture and dolls; and the **kitchen**, where she would make tea (very milky English-style, with cream, in cups and saucers) and they'd play coin games on the kitchen floor
- Mrs. Brown was **English** with a British accent; she called the children "dearie"
- She would leave notes signed "**Mary Poppins**" with a drawing of Mary Poppins
- Once threw a **birthday party** in March for Claire and a neighbor girl Laura whose birthdays were a week apart
- Ford observes: "This is mom. You're describing mom. That's incredible." — Claire's grounding approach to children, her attentiveness with clear boundaries, her love of the pool and communal activity, all trace back to Mrs. Brown
- The other girl (Laura) had a chaotic home life; Mrs. Brown was also an anchor for her

### Faith

- Claire's mother became a **Quaker** after the divorce, attending a Quaker Meeting in Augusta, GA (~13 miles away), once a month; Claire went with her
- Before the divorce, they attended the **Presbyterian Church** in Aiken
- Claire has been deeply involved in **Bible study** throughout her adult life, doing weekly Bible study consistently
- Faith is the "backstop" of everything in her life — navigation, coping, operating
- "It's life giving"
- She distinguishes faith-based friendships from others: with the faith foundation, you can go deeper into life, purpose, vulnerability; without it, there's a ceiling to depth

**Father's death as defining spiritual moment:**
- Claire's father was diagnosed with **leukemia**; went into remission; then came out of remission
- Claire went to Aiken for his final 10 days, with friends watching her kids back in Richmond
- At the very end, he thought he was "getting on a train" and needed to pack
- He looked at Claire and asked: "Are you my Lord and Savior?" Then looked away and said, "No, of course you're not." — that was his last direct eye contact with her
- That night, the **hospice chaplain**, Martha (who was also a friend of her father's), called and asked one question: "Do you need me to come?" Claire said yes; Martha hung up and arrived
- Martha sang to him; then came out and told Amy (his partner) she needed to **let him go** — to go in and tell him it was okay
- Amy did. Then Martha called everyone in. The family gathered around him, and he died peacefully surrounded by them
- "I looked at his body and thought: this is just his vessel. He's gone."
- "It was a God moment. God was just revealing Himself to me."
- Her sister (without faith) was "desperately clinging to the body"; Claire felt at peace
- She came away thinking that all that mattered was **love, hope, peace**
- "I was so heartbroken and sad, but so at peace."

### Education & Young Adulthood

- Attended **University of Colorado** (Boulder) for first year of college
- Bill Prior went with Claire to move her into her dorm; her mother sent him because he could carry heavy things
- Said goodbye to Bill at the Denver airport: "Goodbye, see you at Christmas." Returned to her dorm knowing no one, in a huge university, and "wept in the farewell area of the dorm."
- Then "picked herself up by her bootstraps" and had a great year
- Left Colorado primarily for financial reasons, and partly to be closer to Bill
- Transferred to **Clemson University** — moved in with childhood friend Susan Shulhofer; had a fun year but found nothing at Clemson she wanted to major in
- Transferred to **University of South Carolina** (Columbia) — majored in **International Studies**, minored in **French**
- Describes herself as a "loner" during college years — a product of her childhood environment (her mother didn't encourage friends over; an isolated, self-sufficient child became a self-sufficient young adult)
- Later became BTA President and was involved in activities; the loner tendency faded in adult community life

### Marriage, Richmond, and Raising Kids

- Married Bill in her mid-20s; moved to **Richmond, Virginia** together
- "So exciting, so fun to get away from all that was inhibiting us — Dad had his share too"
- "I didn't have to sneak to see my dad anymore"
- **Corey** born when Claire was ~27 — "having a baby is amazing"
- Went back to work briefly after Corey; stayed home after a few months and was very happy to do so
- **Ford** born when Claire was ~30
- **Morgan** born when Claire was ~33
- Lived at 1307 Severn Road (their first house in Richmond), then moved to **9104 University Boulevard** (where the kids grew up) — they lived at University Boulevard from Claire's late 30s through much of their family life
- Substituted at **Tuckahoe Elementary** for years while kids were young
- When Morgan started middle school, went to work full-time (but still part-time hours) at **St. Christopher's School** — left right after Morgan's bus each morning, home when kids were home
- Found great fulfillment in that work: "I loved getting back to work and feeling like I was contributing"

**Ages 40-50 ("good years, fun years"):**
- Corey in college, Ford and Morgan in high school; beach trips every year; lots of sports
- Was always home when kids got off the school bus
- "I loved it"

**Ages 53-63 (recent decade at time of recording):**
- Lost: her mother, her father, Amy (Bill's step-mother or close family figure?), Bill's mom, Bill's dad, Bill's sister
- Gained: all three kids married; babies born (grandchildren)
- "Lots going on between 53 and 63 — remarkable loss, but also remarkable life."

### Advice for Young Couples

1. **Find a faith community.** Echoes her father's advice to her and Bill when they first moved to Richmond. "I think it's foundational to life." Friendships grounded in faith go deeper — more vulnerable, more able to engage with purpose and hard questions.
2. **Drink moderately.** (Said with a laugh.)

### On Being "Normal" After a Difficult Childhood

Ford asks: people have always said, "Why are you so normal?" — how?

Claire's answer:
- Partly **God's grace**
- Partly **people**: Eva, Mrs. Brown, her father, her sister Deb, the families at Fermata Club — each a grounding presence
- Partly **awareness**: lying in the polo fields at Fermata Club as a child, looking up at the sky, praying — "All right, Lord. You're with me. I know you're there." Not asking to be delivered; just acknowledging presence. Using those early experiences as a triangulation point: "I will not repeat this. Whatever I do will be organized around never encountering this again."
- Partly **modeling**: watching healthy families at Fermata Club — dads and moms together, kids playing — gave her a picture of what she wanted

### Parenting Philosophy

On Ford's difficult period in 10th grade (narcolepsy diagnosis, low point):
- Claire was "concerned, not deeply worried"
- Understood it as a teenager going through normal struggles compounded by narcolepsy
- She and Bill were in sync: didn't push Ford into a box; tried to make sure he knew he was loved and secure; set healthy limits but let him make mistakes
- "I think I just tried to find opportunities to make sure you knew you were loved and secure."
- Did notice some "shady behavior" from time to time; brief periods of concern; never catastrophic

On Ford's Leslie exploration (neighbor; discovered via AOL Instant Messenger left open on computer screen):
- "That was alarming."
- Had a talk with Ford; Ford processed it and stopped
- "Kids are going to make mistakes."

On how she wants to be remembered by great-grandchildren:
> "Someone who just loved her family — not just loved her family, but knew how to love and support your family... And a woman of faith — that's pivotal. It backstops everything... Someone who loves being outside, loves to read, loves the quiet. Always loved riding my bike, always loved swimming. Love tennis."

## Analysis

Claire Prior's story is one of the most striking examples in the family history of resilience built not through toughness, but through **accumulated loving presence**. She never had a stable mother. What she had instead was a parade of women — Eva, Mrs. Brown, Susan, Deb — who each provided something steady at the moments she needed it most.

Several recurring patterns:

1. **Modeling as formation**: She built her values by watching what she wanted to become, not by being taught. Fermata Club families, Mrs. Brown's structured generosity, Eva's quiet warmth — all were teachers without ever being formal ones.

2. **Faith as the thread**: Her faith isn't intellectual or doctrinal — it's the felt sense of not being alone. The moment of her father's death crystallized it: she could let him go because she believed somewhere was receiving him. That ability to release, grounded in faith, kept her from the grasping that grief can produce.

3. **Parenting as reparation**: She describes herself as "getting this part right" — consciously building a family life that looked like what she'd seen at the Fermata Club, not what she'd experienced on Kershaw Street. The pool, the community, the unconditional presence — these were her deliberate choices.

4. **The loner-made-connector arc**: The child who had no neighborhood friends became the BTA president and the woman who builds community with tenacity and warmth. Isolation in childhood taught her to treasure connection as an adult.

5. **Boundaries as love**: Her ability to set firm limits with her own mother (once she was geographically free) and to support her kids doing the same is notable given that she grew up with no model for healthy limits.

## Appendix

### On Mrs. Brown (Extended)

Mrs. Brown stands out as one of the most formative figures in Claire's early life. Ford's immediate reaction: "This is mom. You're describing mom." The parallels are striking:
- Both bring structured love with clear, unsentimental limits (Mrs. Brown's "half past five, everyone leaves")
- Both make the child feel seen and special in a bounded, sustainable way — not overwhelming, not withheld
- Both are active, outdoor, community-centered
- Both have an English sensibility (Mrs. Brown was actually English)
- Both leave lasting impressions through small, specific rituals (tea and coin games; cookies and milk; birthday party at the dining table)

Ford's articulation: "I have a new life goal — to be this person."

### On Faith and Her Father's Death

Claire's account of her father's death is one of the most detailed and emotionally rich passages in the recording. Key details:
- Hospice chaplain Martha asking only "Do you need me to come?" — and then just arriving
- Martha singing to him
- The instruction: "Tell him he can go"
- Everyone gathered around, him dying peacefully surrounded by family
- Claire's dissociation between the body ("just his vessel") and her father's actual presence
- The contrast with her non-believing sister clinging to the body

This experience crystallized her faith not as doctrine but as **felt knowledge**: she knew something was real because she experienced something she couldn't otherwise explain.

### On Her Father's Parting Wisdom

> "When my dad was sick with cancer and I wanted to just be with him, he made it very clear: 'Claire, I'm not your priority. Your family is — Bill, your kids. That's your priority. Not me. Not your mother. You don't owe us anything.' Not every parent would say that."
